# Media Attention Measurement (Attention Quality & Signal Strength)

## Overview
Media Attention Measurement is a methodology used to assess **whether advertising and media placements actually earn human attention**, how that attention is distributed across time and elements, and whether it supports communication and brand objectives. It is applied when reach and impressions are insufficient proxies for effectiveness, particularly in cluttered, skippable, or multitasking environments.

This methodology focuses on **attention quality, not exposure quantity**, and is designed to diagnose why media works or fails before, during, or after campaigns.

## Core Principles
- **Attention is not binary.** Exposure does not equal attention; attention varies in intensity and duration.
- **Quality matters more than volume.** Brief, focused attention can outperform longer, distracted exposure.
- **Attention must be attributable.** Measurement must link attention to specific media, creatives, and moments.
- **Context shapes attention.** Platform, format, environment, and task load materially affect outcomes.
- **Attention is a means, not an end.** Attention must support message processing and brand impact.
- **Comparability requires standardization.** Attention metrics must be defined and measured consistently.

## Survey Design Requirements

### Question Structure
- **Always anchor attention to a specific exposure window.**
  - Define platform, format, and timeframe clearly.
- **Use a layered attention framework:**
  1. Opportunity to see (OTS)
  2. Attention capture
  3. Attention duration
  4. Attention focus (what was noticed)
  5. Cognitive or emotional processing
- **Sequence questions to avoid priming:**
  1. Unaided recall of ads or content
  2. Aided recognition of specific executions
  3. Attention and engagement diagnostics
  4. Message and brand takeout
- **Separate attention from liking.**
  - Measure attention before any evaluative judgments.
- **Isolate creative-level diagnostics.**
  - If multiple assets ran, evaluate each independently.
- **Include context-of-exposure questions.**
  - Device, multitasking, sound on/off, placement.
- **Avoid leading descriptors.**
  - Do not describe the ad before measuring attention.

### Scale Design
- **Use 5-point attention intensity scales.**
  - Example: “Not at all” → “A great deal”
- **Use time-based self-report cautiously.**
  - Prefer bounded ranges (e.g., “less than 2 seconds”, “2–5 seconds”, “5+ seconds”).
- **Maintain consistent attention scales across assets.**
- **Avoid agreement scales for attention.**
  - Attention is experiential, not attitudinal.
- **Use categorical indicators for focus.**
  - What elements were noticed first or most.

### Sample Questions
**Unaided Recall**
- “What ads or sponsored content do you remember seeing recently while using [platform]?”
  - Open-ended

**Aided Recognition**
- “Do you recall seeing this ad while using [platform]?”
  - Yes / No / Not sure

**Attention Capture**
- “How much did this ad capture your attention?”
  - 1 = Not at all
  - 5 = A great deal

**Attention Focus**
- “What did you notice first when this ad appeared?”
  - Visuals
  - Headline or text
  - Brand or logo
  - Offer or price
  - Nothing in particular

**Context Diagnostic**
- “What were you doing at the same time as this ad appeared?”
  - Only watching/reading
  - Light multitasking
  - Heavy multitasking

## Common Mistakes to Avoid
- **Equating recall with attention.**
  - *Wrong:* Assuming recalled ads received high attention.
  - *Correct:* Measuring attention intensity separately.
- **Over-relying on self-reported duration.**
  - Duration without focus is misleading.
- **Ignoring context effects.**
  - Attention varies dramatically by platform and usage mode.
- **Mixing creative evaluation into attention measurement.**
  - Liking and persuasion come after attention.
- **Averaging attention across formats.**
  - Attention must be format- and placement-specific.
- **Treating attention as a KPI in isolation.**
  - Attention without message uptake is incomplete.

## Analysis & Output Requirements
- **Primary attention metrics typically include:**
  - Attention capture rate (noticed vs not noticed)
  - Attention intensity score
  - Attention duration bands
  - Element-level focus distribution
- **Analyze attention by context:**
  - Platform
  - Device
  - Sound on/off
  - Multitasking level
- **Link attention to downstream outcomes.**
  - Message comprehension
  - Brand lift
  - Behavioral intent
- **Sample size guidance:**
  - Minimum 200–300 exposed respondents per asset
  - Larger samples for fragmented media plans
- **Use comparative benchmarks.**
  - Compare attention scores across creatives and formats.
- **Avoid false precision.**
  - Attention measures are directional and comparative.
- **Report attention decay where possible.**
  - Early vs late exposure effects.

## Integration with Other Methods
- **Ad Testing:** Use attention diagnostics to explain performance differences.
- **Campaign Effectiveness & ROI:** Attention is an input to impact, not a substitute.
- **Media Planning:** Inform format, placement, and frequency decisions.
- **Message Testing:** Determine whether key messages are actually noticed.
- **Brand Tracking:** Contextualize brand KPI movement with attention quality.

## Quality Checklist
- [ ] Attention is measured separately from recall and liking
- [ ] Exposure context is clearly defined and captured
- [ ] Scales are consistent across assets and formats
- [ ] Element-level focus is explicitly diagnosed
- [ ] Results are analyzed by platform and context
- [ ] Attention is linked to message and brand outcomes
- [ ] Outputs inform creative and media optimization
- [ ] Method avoids treating attention as an end-state metric
